# Writing like a human, not an LLM

When you compose a comment, reply, DM, or post that a person will be seen to have written, it should
read like *them*. Model-sounding text stands out to readers and to platforms, so the aim is a real
voice. This is the voice guide; pair it with `--human` typing (`text-input.md`) and the
confirm-before-post step.

## What a real voice does

Start here — most of writing human is doing these, and the model tells fall away on their own once
you do.

- **Lead with the take or the experience.** "had this exact problem — it was the cache." "honestly
  the battery is the only real downgrade."
- **Be specific and personal.** A concrete detail — a number, a version, a place, a small story —
  reads real, where vague universal advice reads generated.
- **An imperfect register is fine.** lowercase starts, "tbh", "ngl", "imo", a trailing "lol", a
  sentence fragment. One typo reads more human than none — a light touch, not dialect cosplay.
- **Keep it short.** Most real comments are 1–3 sentences; if you wrote a paragraph, it's usually
  twice as long as it needs to be.
- **Match the room.** Read 2–3 nearby comments first (snap the thread) and mirror their length,
  formality, and slang — a terse subreddit reads nothing like a LinkedIn post.
- **React, don't summarize.** Give an opinion about the post rather than restating it.

## Calibrate to context

- **Reddit/forums:** casual, opinionated, often lowercase, slang ok, sub-specific in-jokes. Brevity wins.
- **Messaging (WhatsApp/DM):** very short, contractions, maybe an emoji, no greeting if mid-thread.
- **Professional (email/LinkedIn):** complete sentences, but still warm and direct rather than formal
  and hollow — and still free of scaffolding and buzzwords.

## Catching the model tells in your own draft

Once you've written something, reread it and you'll usually spot a few giveaways. Each has a plain-er
human version right beside it:

- **Throat-clearing** → cut it. "Great question!", "Absolutely!", "I think it's worth noting", "It's
  important to remember", "In conclusion" all add nothing; start at the point.
- **Essay scaffolding** → drop it. "Firstly… Secondly… Finally" and a thesis-body-summary on a 2-line
  comment is structure no person uses for a quick reply; say the thought and stop.
- **Both-sides hedging** → pick a side. "While X has its merits, Y also offers benefits" is a model
  fence-sit; a person says "nah, Y's just better."
- **The em-dash-and-tricolon rhythm** → vary it. "It's fast, it's clean, and it's reliable" and a
  steady stream of em-dashes read AI; plain commas, periods, and the occasional fragment read human.
- **A moralizing wrap-up** → just end. "Ultimately, it depends on your needs" is a closer no one
  needs.
- **Over-completeness** → answer the one thing asked, not the five adjacent things a model would also
  cover.
- **Fingerprint vocabulary** → swap for plain words. "delve", "leverage", "robust", "seamless",
  "boasts", "elevate", "testament to", "navigate the landscape", "in today's world" all read as
  model.

## Before you type, self-check

1. Would a person bother writing this much? (If not, cut it down.)
2. Does it open with filler? (The first sentence is usually throat-clearing — delete it.)
3. Any fingerprint word or tricolon rhythm? (Say it plainly instead.)
4. Is there one concrete, specific detail? (If not, add a real one — or it's probably not worth posting.)
5. Does it match the surrounding comments' vibe? (Snap the thread and compare.)

## Examples

- ❌ "Great point! This is a really insightful take. It's worth noting that there are several
  factors to consider here, and ultimately it depends on your specific use case."
- ✅ "depends what you're running — for me the 16gb was the bottleneck way before the cpu was"

- ❌ "Thank you for sharing! I've found that establishing a consistent routine can be a robust
  solution that helps you seamlessly navigate these challenges."
- ✅ "what fixed it for me was just doing it first thing before i could talk myself out of it"

Then route through `--human` typing, snap to verify the text landed, and **stop for the user's
go-ahead before posting** — composing is yours to do, publishing is theirs to approve.
